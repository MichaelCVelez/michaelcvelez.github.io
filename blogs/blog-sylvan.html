Teaching the
Next Generation

By Michael • August 20, 2020
⚲ El Paso, TX
Scroll

Sylvan Learning Center

School was OUT for the summer! Everyone remembers how great of a feeling that once was. Unfortunately, I had procrastinated on applying for internships or gaining any real world experience in my field. What could I do with my Computer Science degree in El Paso?

Let me preface by saying, I’m awkward as hell with kids. I might be being overly critical of myself, but I find it hard to interact with them because I want to address them as adults. I feel like “baby talking” to kids undermines their true intelligence. So what was I thinking applying to be a Coding and Robotics instructor Coding for kids in grades K-12 at Sylvan Learning Center?! Well, to be honest, I’ve always loved computers and building. I grew up obsessively collecting LEGO Bionicle sets and when I wasn’t playing with those, I was playing a video game or on the family computer (probably on Bionicle.com). Seeing the flier for the job made me think, wouldn’t I have loved something like this when I was that age?

I know for a fact that I was more nervous to meet my students than they were to meet me. They were probably overwhelmed with excitement! But up until now I had no experience with anyone that young. No nieces, nephews, cousins. If there was ever a baby in my family I would be the last one to volunteer to hold it out of fear of dropping it!

I fully believe teaching at Sylvan brought out the fatherly figure that is in me almost immediately. I learned that today’s youth are incredibly intelligent and hilarious. They are knowledge sponges and continuously show so much potential in life. There was one or two class clowns but they showed me the utmost respect when I had to raise my voice or settle them down. They really just wanted to make friends, impress the teacher and complete their assignments.

The lessons in robotics consisted of education brand LEGO sets that included gears, motors, and motion sensors. The sets ranged from drinking birds, a fully rotating Ferris wheel, jumping frogs, roaring lions and many others. After building the set and hooking the pieces up to a tablet, the kids would drag and drop coding blocks on screen in order to make the set move. These included common programming patterns like for loops, while loops, breaks, returns and if statements.

For the second part of the camp, the students would program their very own online games at Tynker.com. It was pretty impressive software while still being fully accessible to their diverse age group. I would be lying if I said I didn’t want to hop on and make my own games as well.

It brought true joy to me knowing that I could be helping inspire the next generation of programmers during this summer camp. I saw a lot of myself in those students and found a new appreciation for kids of all ages. I hope that one day I have my own kid to inspire and share the same memories with.
